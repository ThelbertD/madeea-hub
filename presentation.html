Preparation

Smart Meeting Prep Packets

Before a meeting, the Hub assembles everything relevant into a single page — so nobody walks in cold or spends twenty minutes digging through four sections.

  • Who you're meeting, and whether they're a client or internal.
  • Their last email, last meeting and last completed task.
  • Open action items and recent documents for that client.
  • An AI brief that reads it all and tells you what matters.

The design call: the raw context is assembled first, on the device, and only that is sent to the model. It can't invent a client or a deadline that isn't in your data — and if a meeting has no match, the packet shows what it has rather than erroring.

Service quality

Response-Time SLA

Responsiveness is the service. This measures it per client — average time to first reply, the oldest email still waiting, and whether it's getting better or worse.

  • On Track / At Risk / Breached, with thresholds you set.
  • Oldest unanswered email, surfaced before the client has to chase.
  • A trend arrow: are we slipping with this client, or improving?

The design call: time is counted in working hours. An email arriving 6pm Friday and answered 9am Monday costs one hour, not sixty-three — the metric measures responsiveness, not weekends. An unanswered email is never averaged in as a zero; it's reported separately as an open clock.

Capture

Voice-to-Task

Between meetings, speak the thing you must not forget. It comes back as a structured task — title, due date, client, priority — for you to confirm before it saves.

  • “Remind me to send Priya the board pack by Friday, it's urgent.”
  • Parsed into an editable card. Nothing saves until you press Save.
  • Falls back to typing when the browser can't listen, or you'd rather type.

The design call: the model reads intent and writes a clean title — but it is never trusted with dates. “Friday” is resolved by deterministic code against the real calendar; a date in the past, or over a year out, is discarded rather than saved. A task silently due on the wrong day is worse than a task with no date.

Reliability

Follow-up Nudges

The emails that fall through the cracks aren't the ones in your inbox — they're the ones you sent and nobody answered. They sit in Sent, invisible, forever.

  • Dead threads: mail you sent that got no reply after N days.
  • Stale tasks: started, then untouched and forgotten.
  • Each one says why it's flagged, with Follow up or Snooze.

The design call: it deliberately does not re-flag unanswered incoming mail — that's already an SLA breach, and nagging you twice about one problem under two different names is exactly how people learn to ignore alerts.

Accountability

Client Activity Log

“What did we actually do for this client last month?” — answered in one tab, instead of reconstructed from three sections and a memory.

  • Every task, email and meeting on one timeline, newest first.
  • Grouped by Today / This week / Last week / month.
  • Filter by type and date range; click any entry to open the real item.

The design call: a task appears twice — when it was created and when it was completed. Those answer different questions, and “what did we deliver” is almost always the second one. Emails appear in both directions, so the record shows the conversation, not a client emailing into a void.

Team

Multi-EA Delegation

Assign work to a named person, see at a glance who is buried and who has room, and keep a record of who moved what onto whose plate.

  • An assignee on every task card — click to reassign.
  • Filters: My Tasks, All Tasks, Unassigned, or one person.
  • A workload view: open, overdue, urgent, completed this week.

The design call: the person who created a task and the person it's for are different people — so reassigning never rewrites authorship. And every reassignment is logged by the database itself, not by the app, so it's recorded no matter which route it took. An audit trail the software can forget to write isn't an audit trail.

How it was built

Every control was clicked before this deck was written.

Features were driven in a real browser, not assumed to work. That sweep found things nobody would have caught by eye.

  • 187 controls clicked across all 13 pages, watching for errors.
  • A render loop on the Tasks board — invisible, but burning CPU on the busiest page.
  • 14 buttons that looked live and did nothing. All now work.

A button that appears to work and doesn't is worse than no button at all — it teaches people to distrust the tool.
